M11 — Wild Duck Cluster

Captured: 2025‑09‑14 • Telescope: Celestron Origin

  • Exposure: 10s × 60 (10m)
  • Settings: ISO 200
  • Filters: Clear
  • Mount: EQ Mode
  • Processing: PixInsight → Adobe Lightroom
  • Location: Bortle 4, Colorado

Messier 11, nicknamed the Wild Duck Cluster, is a rich open star cluster located about 6,000 light-years away in the constellation Scutum. It contains nearly 3,000 stars packed into a relatively small region, making it one of the densest and most impressive open clusters known. The cluster spans about 20 light-years across and is estimated to be around 220 million years old. Its name comes from the way its brighter stars form a V-shaped pattern that resembles a flock of ducks in flight.

NGC 253 — Sculptor Galaxy

Captured: 2025‑09‑14 • Telescope: Celestron Origin

  • Exposure: 10s × 261 (43m 30 sec)
  • Settings: ISO 200
  • Filters: Clear
  • Mount: EQ Mode
  • Processing: PixInsight → Adobe Lightroom
  • Location: Bortle 4, Colorado

NGC 253, also called the Sculptor Galaxy or Silver Coin Galaxy, is a bright spiral galaxy about 11 million light-years away in the constellation Sculptor. It’s a “starburst” galaxy, meaning new stars are forming at a much higher rate than normal. The galaxy spans roughly 90,000 light-years across — a little smaller than our Milky Way — and is filled with dusty lanes and glowing star-forming regions. Because it’s nearby and very bright, it’s one of the easiest galaxies outside the Local Group to spot with small telescopes.
